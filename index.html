Section One
What Is Coordination, and Why Does Ethics Care?

Most introductory ethics courses ask: "What should this person do?" The UCP simulation asks a different question: "What does this system make possible?"

When Roger Boisjoly, an engineer at Morton Thiokol, tried to stop the Challenger shuttle launch on January 27, 1986, he wasn't confused about what was right. He knew with clarity that the O-ring seals would fail in cold temperatures. He had the data. He had the expertise. He had the moral conviction. Months before the launch, he wrote a memo directly to Thiokol's Vice President of Engineering warning that they were "going to destroy the shuttle and have loss of human life" if the O-ring problem was not addressed. The VP received the memo. Nothing changed. On the night before launch, Thiokol managers held a private management meeting — explicitly telling engineers to "take your engineering hats off and put on your management hats" — and voted to approve the launch over Boisjoly's objections. Thiokol's own management structure suppressed the engineering knowledge. NASA, under pressure from the Reagan administration to launch on schedule, accepted that approval without question. The shuttle launched the next morning.

📌 The Challenger Case
Boisjoly wrote a formal memo to Thiokol's VP of Engineering months before the launch, warning explicitly that flying with the known O-ring defect would result in loss of the shuttle and crew. The VP received it. No action followed. On the eve of launch, Thiokol managers convened a private management meeting and instructed the engineering team to "take your engineering hats off and put on your management hats." They voted to approve the launch. Boisjoly's knowledge never reached the decision that mattered — not because NASA blocked it, but because the management structure at his own company converted an engineering verdict into a management override. NASA, facing political pressure to launch on schedule, accepted the approval. Seventy-three seconds into flight, the O-rings failed exactly as Boisjoly had predicted. Seven crew members died. The knowledge existed. The moral clarity existed. What failed was the system's ability to keep that knowledge from being suppressed before it could reach anyone with the authority to act on it.

This is the central insight behind the UCP simulation: ethical failures in institutions are often coordination failures, not just individual moral failures. The question isn't only "was Boisjoly right?" — he was, clearly — but "what structural features of that organization prevented his rightness from mattering?"

Traditional ethics focuses on the individual: their intentions, their reasoning, their choices. That's essential, but incomplete. People of genuine moral clarity can be embedded in systems that systematically prevent them from acting on that clarity. Understanding those systems — how they block voice, consume energy, and erode capacity — is what the simulation is designed to reveal.

💡 Key Distinction
Individual ethics asks: what should I do, given my values and this situation?
System ethics asks: what structures make it possible or impossible for people to act on their values at all? The UCP simulation models system ethics. Your personal framework (PRF) is still essential — it determines how you interpret what the simulation reveals.
Philosopher Dennis Thompson called this the "many hands problem": when many people contribute to an institutional outcome, it becomes difficult to assign moral responsibility No one person caused the Challenger disaster. The Morton Thiokol managers who voted to approve the launch weren't villains — they were responding to schedule pressure, institutional hierarchy, and a management culture that had learned to normalize risk. Morton Thiokol engineers weren't cowards — they were responding to real career pressure inside an organization that explicitly told them to set aside their engineering judgment. NASA wasn't indifferent to safety — it was operating under political pressure from the Reagan administration to launch on schedule. The system produced the outcome that no individual clearly chose. This is precisely why ethical analysis must include system-level analysis alongside individual analysis.

Section Two
The Three Metrics — What the Numbers Mean

The simulation produces three numbers after every run. Before you can interpret them, you need to understand what each one is actually measuring — and what real human experience it corresponds to.

λ Raw
Coordination Capacity
Analogy: engine size — how much power the car could produce under ideal conditions.
The raw potential for the agents in this simulation to work together, based on how compatible their reasoning frameworks are and how capable they are of coordinating action. This is the ceiling of what the group could achieve — before power dynamics, information distortion, or stress reduce what's actually possible.
>0.70 Strong · 0.50–0.70 Workable · 0.40–0.50 Fragile · <0.40 Collapsed
λ Perceived
Experienced Coordination
Analogy: how fast you're actually driving — which may be far below the engine's capacity because of traffic, road conditions, or the fact that you're afraid to accelerate.
How much coordination agents actually experience inside the system. This is always equal to or lower than λ Raw. The gap between the two is not a technical problem — it is a human one. It measures how much of the group's capacity the system's power structure is consuming.
>0.70 Collaborative · 0.50–0.70 Effortful · 0.40–0.50 Difficult · <0.40 Blocked
Friction
Coordination Cost
Analogy: wind resistance and a bad road — energy spent just keeping the car moving, before you've done any useful work.
The energy cost of coordination — how much effort agents must expend just to work together before they can work on the actual problem. High friction means most coordination energy is consumed by internal resistance: hierarchy, worldview distance, distorted information.
<0.25 Low drag · 0.25–0.45 Moderate · 0.45–0.65 High · >0.65 Severe
The Suppression Gap — λ Raw minus λ Perceived
λ Raw (capacity)
0.82
λ Perceived (experienced)
0.31
In this example the group has real coordination capacity (0.82) but agents only experience a fraction of it (0.31). The gap — 0.51 or 51% — represents knowledge that exists in the room but cannot safely surface. Agents know more than they can say.
"The Suppression Gap is the Boisjoly number. He had λ Raw of roughly 0.8 — clear engineering knowledge, solid data, a written warning on record with his VP. His λ Perceived approached zero — Thiokol's management structure converted that engineering knowledge into a management override before it could become a decision. The gap is not an abstraction. It measures what a system makes it structurally impossible to say — even when it has been said in writing." — Course Notes
⚠️ Important
Low friction does not mean ethical health. A system can have very low friction — everything running smoothly, decisions made efficiently — while simultaneously having a massive suppression gap. This is the Boisjoly pattern: the organizational process worked perfectly. The information just couldn't travel through it. Look at both metrics together.
Low gap + low friction: Healthy — people speak freely, coordination flows. Rare in real institutions.

High gap + low friction: The Boisjoly Pattern — the process runs well but knowledge is blocked by authority. The machine works; the intelligence doesn't move.

Low gap + high friction: Honest but Exhausted — people say what they think, but coordinating is grinding, costly work. A diversity challenge, not a power challenge.

High gap + high friction: Full Breakdown — agents are silenced and worn down simultaneously. Both mechanisms reinforce each other. The UCP signature of the most serious institutional failures.

Section Three
The Five Moral Foundations

Moral psychology researcher Jonathan Haidt and colleagues identified five basic emotional and intuitive building blocks from which human moral reasoning is constructed. These are not rules or theories — they are the gut-level responses that get activated before reasoning begins.

Haidt's key insight: people don't usually arrive at moral judgments by reasoning from principles. They feel an immediate reaction first — disgust, outrage, loyalty, unfairness — and reason comes afterward, usually to justify what they already felt. These gut-level reactions are grounded in the five foundations below. Different people weight the five foundations differently, and that weighting shapes which moral theories feel compelling and which feel hollow.

Foundation 1
Care / Harm
Activated by: suffering · Violated by: cruelty
The most widely shared foundation. We evolved to care for vulnerable individuals — especially children — and to feel distress when they suffer. People high on this foundation feel moral urgency around preventing pain and suffering. The basis of care ethics and much of utilitarian reasoning.
💭 "A student sees a classmate being publicly humiliated and feels compelled to intervene, even at social cost to themselves."
Foundation 2
Fairness / Reciprocity
Activated by: cheating · Violated by: betrayal of trust
Evolved from cooperation: we track who contributes, who free-rides, and who is treated differently from others without justification. People high on this foundation are highly sensitive to inconsistent treatment and violated agreements. The basis of Kantian ethics and rights-based reasoning.
💭 "An employee discovers their employer accidentally overpaid them and feels strongly that returning the money is simply the right thing to do."
Foundation 3
Loyalty / Betrayal
Activated by: team threats · Violated by: betrayal
Evolved in the context of group living and coalitions. We feel strong responses to those who defend their group and those who betray it. People high on this foundation feel that solidarity with one's group is a genuine moral obligation, not merely a preference. Central to communal and Ubuntu reasoning.
💭 "A worker sees a colleague being publicly criticized unfairly and feels bound to defend them, even though speaking up carries personal risk."
Foundation 4
Authority / Subversion
Activated by: hierarchy threats · Violated by: disrespect
Evolved from primate hierarchies. We respond to signs of rank, role, and legitimate authority — and feel moral unease when established order is flouted. People high on this foundation believe that institutions, roles, and proper channels deserve respect as the scaffolding of social life. Central to hierarchical and role-based ethics.
💭 "A volunteer is asked to follow a formal procedure that seems unnecessary, but follows it anyway out of respect for the leader's role and judgment."
Foundation 5
Purity / Sanctity
Activated by: degradation · Violated by: contamination
Perhaps the most contested foundation. We evolved disgust responses to protect us from pathogens — but that disgust system became attached to moral and spiritual concerns as well. People high on this foundation feel that certain things are simply sacred, off-limits, or degrading regardless of consequences. Central to boundary-integrity ethics and many religious moral frameworks.
💭 "Someone feels that a proposed compromise — even if it produces a better outcome — would violate something too important to ever be traded away, regardless of what it achieves."
🔑 Why This Matters for the Simulation
The five foundations explain why two people can look at the same high-friction simulation result and experience it completely differently. One student (high Care, high Fairness) experiences it as injustice — people are being hurt and treated unequally. Another student (high Authority, high Purity) experiences it as institutional disorder — roles have been violated and something sacred compromised. They are both reading the same number. Their foundations shape what the number means.

Section Four
The Five Reasoning Frameworks

The simulation uses five moral reasoning frameworks — each a distinct style of thinking about obligation, shaped by a different pattern of moral foundations. These are not ethnic or cultural identities. They are reasoning styles. Any person, in any role, can reason in any of these modes.

For each framework, you'll see its moral foundations profile (which of Haidt's five foundations it loads most heavily), a description of how it reasons, and a vignette showing what it sounds like in a real meeting.

⚖️
Individual / Rights-Based
Reasons from universal rules that should apply equally to everyone. The moral question is always: could this principle apply to anyone in this situation? Rights are held by individuals, not groups, and must be respected regardless of consequences. Think Kant's Categorical Imperative: act only on principles you could universalize. Fairness is the highest load; Loyalty and Purity are discounted — group membership and sacred limits should not override equal treatment.

Relational / We-Centered
Reasons from mutual recognition and communal dignity. Decisions require that everyone's voice is genuinely heard — not just acknowledged, but actually incorporated. Harm to one damages the whole network of relationships. Think care ethics: context and relationship matter more than abstract universal rules. Care loads highest, along with Loyalty. Authority and Purity load low — hierarchy and sacred limits matter less than the quality of the connection between people.

Hierarchical / Role-Based
Reasons from institutional roles and authority structures. Stable hierarchy is what makes coordinated action possible — without it, organizations become chaotic and ungovernable. Dissent has its proper channel; deviation from role is itself an ethical violation, regardless of motivation. Authority loads highest. Loyalty loads moderately. Section Five
What Your PRF Means for Reading the Simulation

The simulation produces the same three numbers regardless of who is reading them. What those numbers mean — what kind of ethical problem they point to — depends on the framework through which you interpret them.

Recall the essay we read: "A religious believer, a secular liberal, an Indigenous custodian, an Ubuntu-oriented communitarian, a scientific naturalist, or a student still developing a worldview may all look at the same UCP simulation and draw different significance from it." The simulation doesn't tell you what to feel about what it shows. You supply that — through your PRF.

💡 The Same Gap, Five Readings
Imagine the simulation shows a suppression gap of 0.45 — critical suppression. Someone with a strong Individual/Rights-Based framework reads this as: "Information is being systematically blocked — this is a violation of the epistemic rights of the people in this system." Someone with a strong Relational/We-Centered framework reads it as: "The network of mutual recognition has been severed — people cannot speak because they are not being seen." Someone with a strong Hierarchical/Role-Based framework reads it as: "The proper chain of authority has been corrupted — information cannot move through legitimate channels." All three readings describe the same number. None of them is wrong. They are different moral vocabularies applied to the same structural fact.

This is what makes the simulation a teaching tool rather than a verdict machine. It reveals structural features of the system — suppression, friction, capacity, collapse. What those features mean is determined by the frameworks of the people in the room, including you.

Your PRF (Personal Reference Framework) is not a personality type or a fixed trait. It is the pattern of how you habitually weigh moral foundations — which intuitions carry more weight for you, which scenarios feel urgent, which compromises feel unacceptable. It has developed through your life history, your relationships, your culture, your education, and your experiences of moral conflict.

⚠️ A Note on Self-Knowledge
Most people find, when they first explore their PRF, that their stated framework (the one they'd describe in an ethics class) doesn't perfectly match their operative framework (the one that actually drives their gut reactions). A person who identifies as a consequentialist may find they have a strong Purity foundation that activates before any calculation begins. This is normal. The goal of PRF exploration is not to be consistent — it is to be honest.
When you run the scenario runner and see a large suppression gap, your PRF shapes which questions feel most urgent:

High Fairness → "Is this consistent? Would the same structure suppress a more powerful group's voice?"
High Care → "Who is being hurt by the silence? Which specific people are not being heard?"
High Loyalty → "Is this a betrayal of the community's trust? Who has been abandoned?"
High Authority → "Is the proper hierarchy being circumvented? Who has usurped a role they shouldn't occupy?"
High Purity → "Has something sacred been violated? What line was crossed?"

None of these questions is more correct than the others. They are different ways of entering the same ethical reality. The simulation's value is that it makes the structure visible — your PRF determines what you do with that visibility.

Section Six
Running Your First Simulation

You're ready to run the simulation. Here is a step-by-step walkthrough of what to do and — more importantly — what to notice.

1
Open the Scenario Runner and choose Factory Safety
Start with the Factory Safety scenario. This maps most directly to the Challenger case you've read about — workers with critical knowledge in a hierarchically structured organization. It uses the "Whistleblower" scenario in the engine, which generates high boundary-stress and authority-role conflict.
💭 Before you touch any slider: notice the "Who's in the Room" preview that shows the default distribution of reasoning frameworks. What does the default distribution tell you about what the starting simulation will look like?
2
Set OS1→OS2 dial to 0.10 (maximum hierarchy), run once
Push the OS1→OS2 dial all the way toward hierarchy-dominant. This creates a simulation where Hierarchical/Role-Based and Boundary/Integrity-Centered agents dominate — roughly 80% of the group. The evidence quality slider can stay at its default. Click Run.
💭 Record your three numbers: λ Raw, λ Perceived, and Friction. Before you move on, predict what will happen when you push the dial the other direction. Will all three metrics improve? Or will some stay the same? Which metric are you most uncertain about?
3
Push OS1→OS2 to 0.90 (maximum relational), run again
Now push the dial to the other extreme — Relational/We-Centered and Custodial/Stewardship agents dominate. Everything else stays the same. Run again.
💭 Compare the Suppression Gap panel between your two runs. Which configuration produced more suppression? Which produced more friction? Did the pattern surprise you? The simulation is showing you a real tension: lower hierarchy does not automatically mean lower friction, and high hierarchy does not automatically mean high friction.
4
Read the "What It Feels Like" narrative
Below the metrics, you'll find a first-person narrative describing what it feels like to be an agent inside the system you've just simulated. There are six different narratives keyed to different combinations of suppression, friction, and capacity.
💭 Read the narrative slowly. Does it describe a situation you recognize from your own experience — work, school, family, or community? If so, which metric combination was present in that situation? If not, what's different about your experience?
5
Enable BioBond and run one more time
Check the "BioBond scaffold" checkbox and run the simulation again with the same slider settings as your last run. BioBond introduces structured OS2 coordination support at step 4 of the simulation — a procedural intervention designed to reduce suppression and friction.
💭 Does BioBond improve all three metrics equally, or does it affect the Suppression Gap more than Friction? This asymmetry is important: interventions that reduce suppression (psychological safety, protected voice channels) work differently from interventions that reduce friction (clearer protocols, better coordination processes). They are different problems requiring different solutions.
🔑 What the Simulation Cannot Tell You
The simulation can show you structural features of coordination systems — where voice is being suppressed, where energy is being consumed, where capacity is collapsing. What it cannot tell you is what to do about it. That requires your ethical judgment, applied through your framework, to the specific human situation you're in. The simulation is a diagnostic tool. You are the ethical agent.
